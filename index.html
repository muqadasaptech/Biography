About the Nobel Prize

The Nobel Prize is a set of prestigious international awards established in 1895 by the Swedish chemist and inventor Alfred Nobel, best known for inventing dynamite. The prizes are awarded annually in recognition of outstanding achievements in several categories: Physics Chemistry Physiology or Medicine Literature Peace Economic Sciences (added in 1968, funded by the Swedish central bank) Key Features: Purpose: To honor individuals or organizations that have made significant contributions to humanity in their respective fields. Selection: Committees of experts in each field decide the winners, based on nominations and thorough evaluations. Award Ceremony: Held annually on December 10, the anniversary of Alfred Nobel's death. The ceremonies take place in Stockholm, Sweden (for all categories except Peace) and Oslo, Norway (for the Peace Prize). Each Nobel Prize includes: A gold medal A diploma A monetary award The Nobel Prizes symbolize excellence and global recognition for those who have advanced knowledge, promoted peace, or contributed to the betterment of society.
